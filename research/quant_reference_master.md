## Section 1 — Factor Model Theory: From First Principles

### 1.1 The Capital Asset Pricing Model: Starting Point and Its Failure

Before multi-factor models, the field started with CAPM (Sharpe 1964, Lintner 1965). The core assertion is that the expected excess return of any asset $i$ is proportional to the market's excess return:

$$E[r_i] - r_f = \beta_i \left(E[r_m] - r_f\right)$$

where $\beta_i = \text{Cov}(r_i, r_m) / \text{Var}(r_m)$ measures co-movement with the market portfolio.

CAPM rests on brutal assumptions: investors have homogeneous expectations, no transaction costs, no taxes, and they optimise a mean-variance utility function over a single period. The world fails these assumptions in almost every dimension.

Empirically, CAPM fails on multiple counts documented from the 1970s onward:
- **Beta-return relationship is flat**: Black, Jensen, Scholes (1972) showed low-beta stocks earn too much and high-beta stocks earn too little vs. CAPM predictions.
- **Size effect**: Banz (1981) showed small-cap stocks earn higher returns after controlling for beta.
- **Value effect**: Stattman (1980), Rosenberg et al. (1985): high book-to-market stocks outperform.
- **Momentum**: Jegadeesh and Titman (1993): past 12-month winners outperform past losers by approximately 1% per month.

These anomalies are not small. They survive across multiple decades, multiple markets, and multiple researchers — ruling out simple data-mining explanations.

### 1.2 The Arbitrage Pricing Theory: Why Multiple Factors

Stephen Ross (1976) developed the Arbitrage Pricing Theory (APT) as a generalisation of CAPM. The core insight is clean: if returns are generated by a linear factor structure, then the absence of arbitrage implies that expected returns must be a linear function of factor betas.

The APT factor model states:

$$r_i = \alpha_i + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \beta_{ik} f_k + \epsilon_i$$

where $f_k$ are $K$ common factors, $\beta_{ik}$ is asset $i$'s loading on factor $k$, $\epsilon_i$ is idiosyncratic noise with $E[\epsilon_i \epsilon_j] = 0$ for $i \neq j$.

If portfolios can be formed that are exposed to exactly one factor (pure factor portfolios), and these portfolios earn risk premia $\lambda_k$, then in the absence of arbitrage:

$$E[r_i] - r_f \approx \sum_{k=1}^{K} \beta_{ik} \lambda_k$$

The APT makes no assumption about investor utility functions or return distributions. It only requires: (1) a linear factor structure for returns, and (2) no arbitrage. This is far weaker than CAPM's assumptions, which is why it is more robust.

The APT does not tell you *which* factors to use — that is an empirical question. This opened the door for the factor zoo that followed.

### 1.3 Fama-French 3-Factor Model

Eugene Fama and Kenneth French (1993) formalised what the anomaly literature had been finding piecemeal. They proposed three factors:

1. **MKT** (Market excess return): $r_m - r_f$
2. **SMB** (Small Minus Big): return of small-cap portfolio minus large-cap portfolio
3. **HML** (High Minus Low): return of high book/market minus low book/market

The model:
$$r_i - r_f = \alpha_i + \beta_i^{MKT}(r_m - r_f) + \beta_i^{SMB} \cdot SMB + \beta_i^{HML} \cdot HML + \epsilon_i$$

**Factor construction methodology**: Fama-French form six portfolios at the intersection of 2 size groups (small, big) and 3 B/M groups (low, neutral, high). Annual rebalancing in June uses December book equity and June market equity. SMB = (SL + SN + SH)/3 − (BL + BN + BH)/3. HML = (SH + BH)/2 − (SL + BL)/2.

The 3-factor model explains most of the size and value anomalies, and reduces alphas on many previously anomalous portfolios. But it leaves several things unexplained, notably momentum.

Carhart (1997) added a **momentum factor (MOM/UMD)**: past 12-month return (skipping the most recent month to avoid short-term reversal), forming the 4-factor model. This is the workhorse model for mutual fund performance evaluation to this day.

### 1.4 Fama-French 5-Factor Model

Fama and French (2015) added two factors to address profitability and investment anomalies documented by Novy-Marx (2013) and others:

4. **RMW** (Robust Minus Weak): return of firms with robust operating profitability minus weak profitability. Profitability = (revenue − COGS − SG&A − interest) / book equity.
5. **CMA** (Conservative Minus Aggressive): return of firms with conservative investment minus aggressive investment. Investment = annual change in total assets / lagged total assets.

$$r_i - r_f = \alpha_i + \beta_i^{MKT} \cdot MKT + \beta_i^{SMB} \cdot SMB + \beta_i^{HML} \cdot HML + \beta_i^{RMW} \cdot RMW + \beta_i^{CMA} \cdot CMA + \epsilon_i$$

**Critical finding**: in the 5-factor model, HML becomes redundant — its role is largely explained by RMW and CMA for typical tests. This is theoretically coherent: value stocks are cheap because they earn low profitability and invest aggressively (growth stocks do the opposite). The value premium is partly a manifestation of the profitability and investment premiums.

**HML remains important** for certain asset classes (e.g., real estate investment trusts, financial stocks) and in international markets where the F-F 5-factor results are less clean.

### 1.5 The q-Factor Model (Hou-Xue-Zhang 2015)

Hou, Xue, and Zhang (2015) derived their factor model from a theoretical investment-based asset pricing framework — the $q$-theory of investment. The key difference from Fama-French is that they start from a production-side (firm) optimisation problem rather than a consumption-side (investor) problem.

In the $q$-model, firms invest until the marginal cost of investment equals the marginal benefit. The first-order condition implies:

$$E[r_i] = \frac{1 + a(I/A)}{q}$$

where $I/A$ is the investment-to-assets ratio and $q$ is Tobin's q. Stocks with lower expected return have higher $q$ (higher price-to-book) and invest more. Stocks with higher profitability have lower implied required return (holding $q$ fixed).

This generates four factors:
- **MKT**: Market excess return
- **ME**: Market equity (size)
- **I/A**: Investment-to-assets
- **ROE**: Return on equity

The q-factor model outperforms Fama-French 5-factor in head-to-head comparisons on many anomaly portfolios (Hou-Xue-Zhang 2015). The intuition is that I/A and ROE more directly map to the theoretical mechanism than HML and CMA.

### 1.6 Risk vs. Behavioural Mispricing: The Great Debate

Every factor premium has two competing explanations. This debate has enormous practical implications for whether the premium will persist.

**Risk-based explanation**: The factor premium compensates investors for bearing systematic risk. For example:
- *Value premium*: Value stocks are distressed, perform badly in bad economic states (Zhang 2005). Investors rationally demand a premium for holding them.
- *Size premium*: Small firms have illiquid equity, concentrated business risk, and higher default probability.
- *Profitability premium*: High profitability firms are relatively safe (low financial distress risk), but their stocks are expensive; the premium exists because investors misprice the *persistence* of profitability.

If risk-based, the premium should be permanent (as long as the risk remains), but may be lower when economic conditions reduce its risk (e.g., value premium compressed during ZIRP due to changed discount rate dynamics).

**Behavioural mispricing explanation**: The factor premium arises from systematic investor errors that create persistent mispricings:
- *Value*: Investors extrapolate recent earnings growth too far into the future (De Bondt and Thaler 1985), overpaying for glamour stocks and underpaying for value stocks.
- *Momentum*: Investors underreact to information (Barberis, Shleifer, Vishny 1998); prices adjust slowly to fundamental news.
- *Low beta*: Investors have a preference for "lottery tickets" — high-beta, high-vol stocks — due to overconfidence and the preference for skewness (Frazzini-Pedersen 2014, BAB factor).

Behavioural explanations imply the premium may be arbitraged away if enough capital chases it — this is the "limits to arbitrage" literature (Shleifer and Vishny 1997). Value's poor performance 2017–2020 has been cited as evidence of crowding.

**Practical implication**: In portfolio construction, risk-based factors warrant permanent allocation; behavioural factors should be monitored for decay signals (IC declining, factor capacity shrinking).

### 1.7 How to Construct a Factor: The Complete Methodology

**Step 1: Define the characteristic**
E.g., Book-to-Market = book equity (last fiscal year) / market cap (month-end). Be precise about timing to avoid look-ahead bias. Use point-in-time data (Compustat quarterly with the correct `rdq` field or equivalent).

**Step 2: Set breakpoints**
Fama-French use NYSE-only breakpoints for size (30th and 70th percentile). Using all-stock breakpoints biases toward small-caps. For a factor like momentum, use all-stock percentiles — the choice depends on what you are trying to capture.

**Step 3: Form portfolios**
Standard approach: quintile or decile portfolios. High–Low (H1−L5) is the long-short factor return.

**Value-weighting vs. Equal-weighting**:
- Value-weighted: each stock weighted by market cap within quintile. Better reflects what a large institutional investor can actually earn; avoids micro-cap domination.
- Equal-weighted: every stock has the same weight. Generates higher gross returns but in assets that are illiquid. IC statistics on equal-weighted tend to look better than what is implementable.

Most academic factor research now uses value-weighting as the standard, though many anomalies are substantially attenuated under VW vs. EW.

**Step 4: Annual vs. monthly rebalancing**
Annual rebalancing is standard for fundamental factors (value, profitability) because the underlying data changes slowly and turnover is lower. Monthly rebalancing for momentum — the signal decays within months. For FX, signals can be reconstructed daily.

**Step 5: Compute factor returns**
$$f_t = \frac{1}{N_H} \sum_{i \in H} w_i r_{it} - \frac{1}{N_L} \sum_{i \in L} w_i r_{it}$$

where $H$ and $L$ are the high and low portfolios, $w_i$ are weights, and $r_{it}$ is the stock return.

```python
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np

def construct_factor(df, characteristic, n_quantiles=5, weight_col='mktcap'):
    """
    Construct long-short factor from a cross-sectional characteristic.
    
    Parameters
    ----------
    df : DataFrame with columns [date, permno, characteristic, ret, mktcap]
    characteristic : str, name of signal column
    n_quantiles : int, number of buckets (5 = quintiles)
    weight_col : str, column for value weighting ('mktcap') or None for EW
    """
    results = []
    for date, g in df.groupby('date'):
        g = g.dropna(subset=[characteristic, 'ret'])
        g['quantile'] = pd.qcut(g[characteristic], n_quantiles, labels=False)
        
        if weight_col:
            g['w'] = g.groupby('quantile')[weight_col].transform(lambda x: x / x.sum())
        else:
            g['w'] = g.groupby('quantile')['ret'].transform(lambda x: 1/len(x))
        
        g['wret'] = g['w'] * g['ret']
        port_ret = g.groupby('quantile')['wret'].sum()
        
        factor_ret = port_ret.iloc[-1] - port_ret.iloc[0]  # top minus bottom
        results.append({'date': date, 'factor_ret': factor_ret})
    
    return pd.DataFrame(results).set_index('date')
```

## Section 2 — Signal Engineering and the IC/ICIR Framework

### 2.1 What is the Information Coefficient?

The **Information Coefficient (IC)** is the cross-sectional correlation between a signal (alpha score, forecast) and the subsequent realised return. It is the most fundamental measure of signal quality in quantitative investing.

$$IC_t = \text{Corr}\left(\hat{\alpha}_{it}, r_{i,t+h}\right) = \frac{\text{Cov}(\hat{\alpha}_{it}, r_{i,t+h})}{\sigma(\hat{\alpha}_{it}) \cdot \sigma(r_{i,t+h})}$$

where the correlation is computed cross-sectionally across assets $i$ at time $t$, and $h$ is the holding horizon. In practice, Spearman rank correlation is preferred over Pearson IC because it is robust to outliers in returns and characteristics.

**Interpreting IC magnitude**:
| IC Range | Interpretation |
|----------|---------------|
| 0.00–0.02 | Barely useful; hard to distinguish from noise |
| 0.02–0.05 | Marginal; usable if breadth is very high |
| 0.05–0.10 | Good; commercially viable signal |
| 0.10–0.20 | Excellent; rare in practice |
| > 0.20 | Extraordinary; check for look-ahead bias |

Monthly momentum ICs across equities typically run 2–5%. Most discretionary stock-picking by mutual fund managers implies an IC well below 0.05 (Grinold 1989).

### 2.2 ICIR: Why It Matters More Than IC Alone

The **IC Information Ratio (ICIR)** is the mean IC divided by the standard deviation of IC over time:

$$ICIR = \frac{E[IC_t]}{\sigma(IC_t)} = \frac{\bar{IC}}{\sigma_{IC}}$$

Consider two signals:
- Signal A: Mean IC = 0.06, σ(IC) = 0.15 → ICIR = 0.40
- Signal B: Mean IC = 0.04, σ(IC) = 0.04 → ICIR = 1.00

Signal B is vastly more reliable despite having lower average IC. In combination portfolios and in real-world position sizing, *consistency* of the signal is what generates risk-adjusted returns. A signal with high ICIR will generate a more stable P&L, fewer drawdowns, and more predictable behaviour.

**Rule of thumb for practical usefulness**:
- ICIR < 0.25: borderline; needs high breadth or combination
- ICIR 0.25–0.50: usable; suitable for inclusion in a multi-signal model
- ICIR > 0.50: strong; can drive meaningful allocation

### 2.3 The Fundamental Law of Active Management

Grinold (1989) and Grinold-Kahn (2000) formalised the relationship between signal quality (IC), breadth (BR), and portfolio performance (IR):

$$IR \approx IC \times \sqrt{BR}$$

where:
- $IR$ = Information Ratio of the actively managed portfolio
- $IC$ = Information Coefficient of the signal
- $BR$ = Breadth — number of **independent** bets per year

This law is one of the most important results in quantitative finance. Its implications are profound:

**Implication 1: Breadth is as important as skill**
If IC = 0.05 and BR = 100 (trading 100 independent positions per year), IR = 0.05 × √100 = 0.5. If you can double breadth to 200 while maintaining the same IC, IR rises to 0.71 — a 42% improvement without finding better signals.

**Implication 2: Frequency and universe expansion are both valid**
For a G10 FX portfolio with 9 pairs, if you rebalance monthly: BR ≈ 9 × 12 = 108 bets/year. If daily rebalancing and signals are genuinely independent day-to-day (which requires testing): BR ≈ 9 × 252 ≈ 2,268. This would give IR = 0.05 × √2268 ≈ 2.4 — though this assumes daily ICs are achievable, which is much harder.

**Implication 3: Independence matters critically**
The law requires *independent* bets. If 7 of your 9 G10 FX pairs are highly correlated (as EUR/USD, GBP/USD, AUD/USD often are), effective breadth might be 4–5 pairs, not 9. Correlation between signals also reduces effective breadth. The correct formula accounting for correlation is:

$$IR \approx IC \times \sqrt{\frac{BR}{1 + (BR-1)\bar{\rho}}}$$

where $\bar{\rho}$ is the average pairwise correlation between bets. As $\bar{\rho} \to 0$, this recovers the original law. As $\bar{\rho} \to 1$, IR → IC × 1 = IC (no benefit from breadth).

**Implication 4: Signal combination**
If you have $K$ uncorrelated signals, each with the same IC, combining them multiplies effective IC by $\sqrt{K}$:

$$IR_{combined} = IC \times \sqrt{K \times BR}$$

This is why multi-signal models nearly always beat single-signal models.

### 2.4 Alpha Decay: Measuring and Fitting

**Alpha decay** is the empirical phenomenon that signal predictive power diminishes over longer horizons. A signal that has IC = 0.06 at 1-month horizon may have IC = 0.03 at 3 months and IC = 0.01 at 6 months.

The standard framework models decay as exponential:

$$IC(h) = IC_0 \times e^{-\lambda h}$$

where $h$ is the horizon, $IC_0$ is the instantaneous IC, and $\lambda$ is the decay rate. The **half-life** is $h_{1/2} = \ln(2) / \lambda$.

**Measuring decay**:
1. Compute IC at horizons $h = 1, 2, 3, ..., H$ months
2. Fit: $\ln(IC(h)) = \ln(IC_0) - \lambda h$ by OLS
3. Extract $\lambda$ and compute half-life

```python
from scipy.optimize import curve_fit
import numpy as np

def measure_alpha_decay(signal_df, ret_df, horizons=[1,2,3,6,9,12]):
    """
    Measure IC at multiple horizons and fit exponential decay.
    
    signal_df : DataFrame [date x asset] of signal values
    ret_df    : DataFrame [date x asset] of forward returns
    """
    ics = {}
    for h in horizons:
        fwd_ret = ret_df.shift(-h)  # h-period forward return
        # Compute cross-sectional rank IC each month
        monthly_ic = signal_df.corrwith(fwd_ret, axis=1, method='spearman')
        ics[h] = monthly_ic.mean()
    
    horizons_arr = np.array(list(ics.keys()))
    ic_arr = np.array(list(ics.values()))
    
    # Fit exponential decay: IC(h) = IC0 * exp(-lambda * h)
    def decay_func(h, ic0, lam):
        return ic0 * np.exp(-lam * h)
    
    popt, _ = curve_fit(decay_func, horizons_arr, ic_arr, p0=[0.05, 0.2])
    ic0, lam = popt
    half_life = np.log(2) / lam
    
    return {'IC0': ic0, 'lambda': lam, 'half_life_months': half_life, 'ic_by_horizon': ics}
```

**Practical implication for rebalancing frequency**: The optimal holding horizon is roughly equal to the alpha half-life. For carry in FX (half-life ~3 months), monthly rebalancing is appropriate. For high-frequency microstructure signals (half-life ~1 day), daily rebalancing is necessary.

### 2.5 Signal Z-Scoring and Winsorisation

**Why cross-sectional standardisation is necessary**: Raw signal values (e.g., forward P/E ratios ranging from 5 to 50 with outliers at 200) cannot be compared across time or combined with other signals. Z-scoring transforms them into a common unit.

Cross-sectional z-score at time $t$:
$$z_{it} = \frac{x_{it} - \mu_t}{\sigma_t}$$

where $\mu_t = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i x_{it}$ and $\sigma_t = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N-1}\sum_i (x_{it} - \mu_t)^2}$ are computed cross-sectionally at each date.

**Why winsorisation is necessary**: Extreme outliers in financial signals (one stock with P/E = 500 in a universe of 300) can dominate the cross-sectional mean and create noise. Standard practice is to cap at ±3σ before or after z-scoring:

$$z_{it}^{win} = \max\left(-3, \min\left(3, z_{it}\right)\right)$$

In FX (9 pairs), winsorisation matters less because the universe is small, but in equity universes of 500–3000 stocks it is essential.

### 2.6 Orthogonalisation: Stripping Factor Exposures

**The problem**: A signal may predict returns not because of its unique information but because it loads on a known risk factor. For example, a "quality" signal (high ROE) may simply be a proxy for low-beta exposure. If we use the signal as-is, we're buying low-beta stocks and attributing the returns to "quality" when it's really just the BAB (Betting Against Beta) factor.

**Orthogonalisation** removes the components of a signal that are explainable by existing factors:

$$\tilde{z}_{it} = z_{it} - \sum_{k=1}^{K} \hat{\gamma}_k \cdot f_{kit}$$

where $\hat{\gamma}_k$ are obtained by regressing $z_{it}$ on factor exposures $f_{kit}$ cross-sectionally.

Mathematical derivation:

Given signal vector $\mathbf{z}_t \in \mathbb{R}^N$ and factor exposure matrix $\mathbf{F}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times K}$:

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\gamma}}_t = (\mathbf{F}_t^{\top} \mathbf{F}_t)^{-1} \mathbf{F}_t^{\top} \mathbf{z}_t$$

$$\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_t = \mathbf{z}_t - \mathbf{F}_t \hat{\boldsymbol{\gamma}}_t = \mathbf{M}_t \mathbf{z}_t$$

where $\mathbf{M}_t = \mathbf{I} - \mathbf{F}_t(\mathbf{F}_t^{\top}\mathbf{F}_t)^{-1}\mathbf{F}_t^{\top}$ is the annihilator (projection) matrix. The residual $\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_t$ is by construction orthogonal to all columns of $\mathbf{F}_t$.

### 2.7 Proper Signal Testing Protocol (No Look-Ahead Bias)

The complete protocol for testing a new signal without look-ahead contamination:

1. **Timestamp discipline**: Signal at time $t$ must use only data available at time $t$. For accounting data: the fiscal year end date is NOT the availability date. Use the SEC filing date (10-Q/10-K). Compustat provides `rdq` (report date of quarterly earnings) — always use this.

2. **Universe definition**: Use the universe as it existed at each point in time. No using today's S&P 500 components to define the historical universe (survivorship bias).

3. **Return measurement**: Align signal date $t$ with return starting at close of $t$ or open of $t+1$. A 1-day lag is conservative and avoids intraday look-ahead.

4. **Expanding-window training**: If any parameters need estimation (e.g., z-score parameters, winsorisation bounds), estimate them only on data available at each point — rolling or expanding window, never full-sample.

5. **The "lag-one extra day" test (AQR standard)**: After computing your strategy returns, also compute them with signals shifted one extra day. If strategy performance drops sharply, you have look-ahead bias — the information is embedded in same-day prices.

6. **Out-of-sample validation period**: Hold out the last 3–5 years of data entirely until the signal is finalised. Only look at the holdout once.

## Section 3 — Portfolio Optimisation: Complete Treatment

### 3.1 Mean-Variance Optimisation: The Mathematics

Markowitz (1952) proposed that portfolio construction should maximise expected return for a given level of risk (variance):

$$\max_{\mathbf{w}} \; \mathbf{w}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\mu} - \frac{\gamma}{2} \mathbf{w}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \mathbf{w}$$

subject to $\mathbf{w}^{\top} \mathbf{1} = 1$ (fully invested), possibly with $w_i \geq 0$ (long-only).

The unconstrained solution (Lagrangian method):

$$\mathbf{w}^* = \frac{1}{\gamma} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \boldsymbol{\mu}$$

This is elegant but fails catastrophically out-of-sample because it requires estimates of $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ (expected returns) and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ (covariance matrix), both of which are measured with substantial estimation error.

**Why MVO fails OOS**:
- **Expected returns** are nearly impossible to estimate. Sample mean returns have standard error $\sigma/\sqrt{T}$. For monthly returns over 5 years (T=60) with σ=15%, the standard error of the mean is 15%/√60 ≈ 1.9% — larger than the expected premium itself for most assets.
- **Covariance matrix inversion** amplifies estimation error. The optimizer "bets" heavily on the most precisely estimated elements, which are noise.
- Michaud (1989): MVO is an "error maximiser" — it overweights assets with the largest positive return estimation errors.

### 3.2 The Estimation Error Problem: Ill-Conditioned Covariance Matrices

For a universe of $N$ assets, the covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ has $N(N+1)/2$ unique elements. Estimating these from $T$ observations requires $T \gg N$.

A rule of thumb: the sample covariance matrix is well-conditioned only when $T > 5N$. For a 500-stock universe, you need 2,500 months of data (200+ years) for a stable covariance estimate. This is obviously impossible.

When $N > T$, the sample covariance matrix is rank-deficient (singular) — it has fewer non-zero eigenvalues than $N$. Even when $N < T$ but the ratio is unfavourable, the smallest eigenvalues are severely biased downward (Marchenko-Pastur distribution), making the inverse wildly unstable.

The condition number $\kappa = \lambda_{max} / \lambda_{min}$ of an ill-conditioned matrix can be $10^6$ or higher, meaning tiny perturbations in the input create massive changes in the output — portfolio weights swing from +200% to -150% with tiny data changes.

### 3.3 Ledoit-Wolf Shrinkage: Mathematical Construction

Ledoit and Wolf (2004) proposed shrinking the sample covariance matrix $\mathbf{S}$ toward a structured target $\mathbf{T}$:

$$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{LW} = (1 - \alpha) \mathbf{S} + \alpha \mathbf{T}$$

where $\alpha \in [0,1]$ is the shrinkage intensity, optimally chosen to minimise the expected Frobenius loss.

**Target matrix options**:

1. **Identity target** ($\mathbf{T} = \bar{\sigma}^2 \mathbf{I}$): Shrinks all correlations to zero, all variances to the cross-sectional mean. Appropriate when you have very few observations and believe assets are roughly independent. Extreme shrinkage.

2. **Constant correlation target**: All pairwise correlations are set to the cross-sectional mean correlation $\bar{\rho}$, while variances are preserved:
   $$T_{ij} = \bar{\rho} \cdot \sigma_i \sigma_j \quad (i \neq j), \quad T_{ii} = \sigma_i^2$$
   This is the most commonly used target for equity portfolios.

3. **Single-factor target**: Target implied by a 1-factor model (CAPM). Appropriate when market risk dominates.

The **Oracle Approximating Shrinkage (OAS)** estimator from Ledoit-Wolf (2020, "Analytical Nonlinear Shrinkage") applies shrinkage *non-linearly* to individual eigenvalues — significantly outperforming the linear formula in realistic settings.

```python
from sklearn.covariance import LedoitWolf
import numpy as np

def shrunk_covariance(returns_matrix):
    """
    Estimate covariance using Ledoit-Wolf analytical shrinkage.
    
    returns_matrix : T x N array of asset returns
    Returns: N x N shrunk covariance matrix
    """
    lw = LedoitWolf().fit(returns_matrix)
    return lw.covariance_, lw.shrinkage_

# For constant correlation target (manual implementation):
def constant_correlation_shrinkage(S, alpha=None):
    """Ledoit-Wolf with constant correlation target."""
    N = S.shape[0]
    # Extract vols and correlations
    vols = np.sqrt(np.diag(S))
    corr = S / np.outer(vols, vols)
    
    # Mean pairwise correlation
    rho_bar = (corr.sum() - N) / (N * (N - 1))
    
    # Construct target
    T = rho_bar * np.outer(vols, vols)
    np.fill_diagonal(T, vols**2)
    
    if alpha is None:
        alpha = 0.5  # default; in practice compute analytically
    
    return (1 - alpha) * S + alpha * T
```

**When to use which target**:
- N << T (e.g., 9 FX pairs, 60 months): Sample covariance is fine; shrinkage helps but modestly. Constant correlation target is appropriate.
- N ≈ T/2 to T: Ledoit-Wolf constant correlation provides meaningful improvement.
- N > T: Must shrink aggressively; consider factor-based covariance models instead.

### 3.4 Black-Litterman: Combining Signals with Equilibrium

Black and Litterman (1990, 1992) addressed the "garbage in, garbage out" problem of MVO by replacing raw expected return estimates with a Bayesian combination of equilibrium returns and investor views.

**Step 1: Equilibrium expected returns**
Start from the market portfolio weights $\mathbf{w}_{mkt}$ (e.g., market capitalisation weights). Reverse-engineer implied expected returns:

$$\boldsymbol{\Pi} = \delta \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \mathbf{w}_{mkt}$$

where $\delta$ is the market risk aversion coefficient (typically $\delta = 2.5$). This gives the vector of expected returns consistent with the market being in equilibrium.

**Step 2: Define views**
An investor specifies $K$ views. Each view is:
- A pick matrix $\mathbf{P} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times N}$ encoding which assets the view is about
- A view return vector $\mathbf{q} \in \mathbb{R}^K$ — what you believe
- A view uncertainty matrix $\boldsymbol{\Omega} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times K}$ — your confidence (diagonal typically)

Example: "I believe EUR/USD will outperform GBP/USD by 50bps/month with uncertainty 20bps." This becomes a row in P and q.

**Step 3: Bayesian update**
$$\boldsymbol{\mu}^{BL} = \left[(\tau \boldsymbol{\Sigma})^{-1} + \mathbf{P}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-1} \mathbf{P}\right]^{-1} \left[(\tau \boldsymbol{\Sigma})^{-1} \boldsymbol{\Pi} + \mathbf{P}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-1} \mathbf{q}\right]$$

where $\tau \approx 0.025$ to 0.05 scales the uncertainty of the equilibrium prior (the prior is less certain than observed returns).

**Step 4: Optimise**
Use $\boldsymbol{\mu}^{BL}$ in standard MVO. The result is much better conditioned than using raw return estimates because the prior pulls extreme positions back toward market weights.

**Using factor signals as views**: Each signal (carry, momentum, value) generates a cross-sectional expected return forecast. Map these to BL views where $\mathbf{P}$ is the long-short factor portfolio and $q = IC \times \sigma_{cross} \times \text{signal\_z-score}$.

### 3.5 Risk Parity and Equal Risk Contribution

**Equal Risk Contribution (ERC)** allocates weights such that each asset contributes equally to total portfolio risk. It avoids the MVO estimation error problem entirely by using only the covariance matrix (not expected returns).

Define the risk contribution of asset $i$:
$$RC_i = w_i \cdot \frac{\partial \sigma_p}{\partial w_i} = w_i \cdot \frac{(\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \mathbf{w})_i}{\sqrt{\mathbf{w}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \mathbf{w}}}$$

ERC solves: $RC_i = RC_j$ for all $i, j$. This is equivalent to:

$$w_i \cdot (\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \mathbf{w})_i = w_j \cdot (\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \mathbf{w})_j \quad \forall i,j$$

There is no closed-form solution; solve numerically:

```python
from scipy.optimize import minimize
import numpy as np

def equal_risk_contribution(cov_matrix):
    """
    Compute Equal Risk Contribution portfolio weights.
    
    cov_matrix : N x N covariance matrix
    Returns: N-vector of weights
    """
    N = cov_matrix.shape[0]
    
    def risk_contributions(w, cov):
        port_var = w @ cov @ w
        marginal_risk = cov @ w
        return w * marginal_risk / np.sqrt(port_var)
    
    def erc_objective(w, cov):
        rc = risk_contributions(w, cov)
        # Minimise sum of squared differences from equal contribution
        target = np.ones(len(w)) / len(w)  # equal risk budget
        return np.sum((rc - target * rc.sum())**2)
    
    constraints = {'type': 'eq', 'fun': lambda w: w.sum() - 1}
    bounds = [(0, 1)] * N  # long-only; remove for long-short
    w0 = np.ones(N) / N
    
    result = minimize(erc_objective, w0, args=(cov_matrix,),
                     method='SLSQP', bounds=bounds, constraints=constraints)
    return result.x
```

**When ERC dominates equal-weight**: When assets have substantially different volatilities and correlations. In G10 FX, JPY pairs have structurally higher vol than EUR pairs; equal-weight over-risks you in JPY. ERC allocates less notional to JPY to equalise risk.

**Risk parity vs. ERC**: "Risk parity" is a broader term often applied across asset classes (equities, bonds, commodities, FX). The idea: bonds have low vol, so lever them up; equities have high vol, lever down. The Bridgewater All Weather and AQR Risk Parity funds operate on this principle.

### 3.6 Hierarchical Risk Parity (HRP)

López de Prado (2016) introduced HRP as a tree-based alternative to MVO that is robust to near-singular covariance matrices. The algorithm has three steps:

**Step 1: Hierarchical clustering**
Compute the correlation matrix. Define a distance metric: $d_{ij} = \sqrt{(1 - \rho_{ij})/2}$. Apply single-linkage hierarchical clustering to create a dendrogram of assets. Assets that are most similar are clustered first.

**Step 2: Quasi-diagonalisation**
Reorder the covariance matrix by the tree structure so correlated assets are adjacent. This makes the matrix "as diagonal as possible" in a hierarchical sense.

**Step 3: Recursive bisection**
Starting from the root, split the portfolio into two sub-clusters. Allocate risk between sub-clusters in inverse proportion to their within-cluster variance:

$$\alpha_1 = 1 - \frac{\tilde{V}_1}{\tilde{V}_1 + \tilde{V}_2}$$

where $\tilde{V}_k$ is the variance of the naive (equal-weight) portfolio within cluster $k$. Recurse until each cluster is a single asset.

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.cluster.hierarchy import linkage, dendrogram, leaves_list
from scipy.spatial.distance import squareform

def hrp_portfolio(cov_matrix, corr_matrix=None):
    """Hierarchical Risk Parity allocation."""
    N = cov_matrix.shape[0]
    if corr_matrix is None:
        std = np.sqrt(np.diag(cov_matrix))
        corr_matrix = cov_matrix / np.outer(std, std)
    
    # Step 1: Hierarchical clustering
    dist = np.sqrt((1 - corr_matrix) / 2)
    dist_condensed = squareform(dist, checks=False)
    link = linkage(dist_condensed, method='single')
    sort_ix = leaves_list(link)
    
    # Step 2: Reorder covariance matrix
    cov_reordered = cov_matrix[np.ix_(sort_ix, sort_ix)]
    
    # Step 3: Recursive bisection
    weights = pd.Series(1.0, index=sort_ix)
    cluster_items = [list(sort_ix)]
    
    while cluster_items:
        cluster_items = [i[j:k] for i in cluster_items
                        for j, k in ((0, len(i)//2), (len(i)//2, len(i)))
                        if len(i) > 1]
        for i in range(0, len(cluster_items), 2):
            c1, c2 = cluster_items[i], cluster_items[i+1]
            v1 = _cluster_var(cov_matrix, c1)
            v2 = _cluster_var(cov_matrix, c2)
            alpha = 1 - v1 / (v1 + v2)
            weights[c1] *= alpha
            weights[c2] *= 1 - alpha
    
    return weights / weights.sum()

def _cluster_var(cov, cluster):
    """Variance of equal-weight portfolio within cluster."""
    sub_cov = cov[np.ix_(cluster, cluster)]
    w = np.ones(len(cluster)) / len(cluster)
    return w @ sub_cov @ w
```

**When HRP beats MVO**: López de Prado's simulations show HRP has lower out-of-sample Sharpe ratio variance than MVO, ERC, and equal-weight when: (a) the covariance matrix is ill-conditioned, (b) there are distinct asset clusters, (c) the number of assets is moderate (20–200). For small N (< 20), the advantage is smaller.

### 3.7 The Kelly Criterion: Derivation from First Principles

John Kelly (1956) derived the optimal bet size for a gambler with log utility. The derivation generalises to portfolio management.

**Bernoulli setting**: A bet wins with probability $p$ (paying odds $b:1$) or loses with probability $q = 1-p$. Maximising expected log wealth:

$$\max_f \; E[\ln W] = p \ln(1 + bf) + q \ln(1 - f)$$

Taking derivative and setting to zero:
$$\frac{pb}{1 + bf} - \frac{q}{1 - f} = 0$$
$$f^* = \frac{pb - q}{b} = p - \frac{q}{b}$$

This is the **Kelly fraction**: the optimal proportion of wealth to bet.

**Continuous-time extension (geometric Brownian motion)**: For an asset with expected excess return $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$:

$$f^* = \frac{\mu}{\sigma^2}$$

This is the **Kelly leverage**: the optimal leverage ratio for a log-utility investor. For a portfolio:
$$\mathbf{f}^* = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \boldsymbol{\mu}$$

Note this is identical to the MVO solution with $\gamma = 1$ (log utility corresponds to risk aversion of 1 in the mean-variance framework).

**Why fractional Kelly (25-50%)?**
1. **Parameter uncertainty**: $\mu$ and $\sigma$ are estimated with error. The true Kelly fraction for the estimated parameters $\hat{\mu}, \hat{\sigma}$ is not the same as for the true parameters. Estimation error biases you toward over-betting.
2. **Drawdown control**: Full Kelly maximises long-run growth rate but implies horrific drawdowns (25–50% drawdowns are routine). Half-Kelly approximately halves drawdowns while costing only a quarter of the growth rate.
3. **Non-stationarity**: Kelly assumes stationary returns. Financial returns are non-stationary; over-betting during adverse regimes can be ruinous.

Standard institutional practice: 25–50% of Kelly. A fund targeting 10% vol with Kelly leverage of 5× would run at 1.25–2.5× leverage.

**Kelly-volatility equivalence**: Targeting volatility at $\sigma_{target}$ is equivalent to setting leverage = $\sigma_{target}/\hat{\sigma}_{portfolio}$. This is approximately half-Kelly when $\sigma_{target} \approx \mu/(2\hat{\sigma})$ — i.e., when the targeted Sharpe ratio is about 0.5.

### 3.8 Practical Comparison: Which Method Wins OOS?

Based on the empirical literature (DeMiguel et al. 2009, López de Prado 2016, Roncalli 2013):

| Method | OOS Sharpe | Drawdown Control | Stability | Implementation |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| 1/N Equal Weight | Baseline | Poor (vol-uncontrolled) | High | Trivial |
| MVO (sample Σ, μ̂) | −0.1 vs. EW | Poor | Low | Complex |
| MVO (Ledoit-Wolf) | +0.1 vs. EW | Moderate | Moderate | Moderate |
| Risk Parity / ERC | +0.15 vs. EW | Good | High | Moderate |
| HRP | +0.12 vs. EW | Good | High | Moderate |
| Black-Litterman | +0.2 to +0.4 vs. EW* | Moderate | Moderate | Complex |
| Kelly (full) | High mean, extreme drawdown | Very Poor | Low | Complex |
| Half-Kelly | +0.15–0.25 vs. EW | Good | Moderate | Moderate |

*BL advantage depends entirely on signal quality; if views are wrong, BL underperforms.

**Recommendation for G10 FX with 9 pairs and 3–5 signals**: Use ERC or HRP for the covariance-based allocation, combined with Black-Litterman to incorporate signal views. This gives the stability of risk-based allocation plus the signal exploitation of BL.

## Section 4 — Risk Management Frameworks

### 4.1 Volatility Targeting: Construction and Evidence

Volatility targeting scales portfolio exposure inversely to realised volatility, maintaining a constant ex-ante risk level:

$$w_t = \frac{\sigma_{target}}{\hat{\sigma}_t} \times w_{base}$$

where $\hat{\sigma}_t$ is the estimated volatility at time $t$ and $\sigma_{target}$ is the desired annualised vol (e.g., 10%).

**EWMA volatility estimation**:
$$\hat{\sigma}_t^2 = (1 - \lambda) r_t^2 + \lambda \hat{\sigma}_{t-1}^2$$

With $\lambda = 0.94$ (RiskMetrics standard), the effective lookback is approximately $1/(1-\lambda) = 16.7$ days. For a 60-day effective window, use $\lambda = 0.967$.

**Rolling window** uses the sample standard deviation of the last $W$ returns. More robust to EWMA's sensitivity to single extreme events but slower to react.

**Moreira-Muir (2017 Journal of Finance)** showed that volatility-managed equity portfolios — scaling down exposure after high-vol periods and scaling up after low-vol periods — generate significant alpha over the static market portfolio. The intuition: expected returns are more stable than realised volatility; when vol spikes (e.g., during crises), the risk-return ratio deteriorates, and reducing exposure improves the risk-adjusted performance.

Key finding: Volatility-managing the market factor adds approximately 0.5 Sharpe ratio points. The effect holds across factors (value, momentum, profitability) and internationally.

```python
def volatility_target(returns, target_vol=0.10, ewma_lambda=0.94, 
                       min_scale=0.2, max_scale=5.0):
    """
    Scale returns to target annualised volatility.
    
    returns : pd.Series of daily returns
    target_vol : annualised target volatility (e.g., 0.10 = 10%)
    """
    # EWMA variance
    ewma_var = returns.ewm(alpha=1-ewma_lambda).var()
    ewma_vol = np.sqrt(ewma_var * 252)  # annualised
    
    # Scaling factor
    scale = target_vol / ewma_vol.shift(1)  # shift to avoid look-ahead
    scale = scale.clip(min_scale, max_scale)
    
    return returns * scale
```

### 4.2 Value at Risk and Conditional VaR

**Value at Risk (VaR)** at confidence level $\alpha$ (e.g., 95% or 99%) is the maximum loss that will not be exceeded with probability $\alpha$ over a holding period:

$$VaR_\alpha = -\inf\{x : F(x) \geq 1 - \alpha\} = F^{-1}(1-\alpha)$$

where $F$ is the return CDF. For a normally distributed portfolio: $VaR_\alpha = \mu - z_\alpha \sigma$ where $z_\alpha$ is the normal quantile (1.645 for 95%, 2.326 for 99%).

**Conditional VaR (CVaR) / Expected Shortfall (ES)** is the expected loss *given* that the loss exceeds VaR:

$$CVaR_\alpha = E\left[-r \mid r < -VaR_\alpha\right] = \frac{1}{1-\alpha} \int_0^{1-\alpha} VaR_u \, du$$

CVaR is the coherent risk measure preferred by regulators post-Basel IV (banks now report ES at 97.5% rather than VaR at 99%). CVaR is:
1. Subadditive: diversification always reduces risk (VaR lacks this property)
2. Convex: optimisation with CVaR constraints is a linear program
3. Better captures tail risk: averages over all losses beyond the threshold

**Historical simulation**: No distributional assumption; compute VaR as the empirical quantile of the historical return distribution. Straightforward but limited by length of history.

**Limitations of VaR/CVaR**:
- Model risk: the distribution assumption is always wrong in the tail
- Liquidity: assumes the position can be liquidated at market price (false in crises)
- Correlation instability: correlations spike in crises, making portfolio-level VaR underestimate true risk
- Gaming: VaR is easy to game by selling out-of-the-money options (premium income inflates Sharpe; downside is deep in the tail, invisible to VaR)

### 4.3 PCA-Based Risk Models: Eigendecomposition

For any $N \times N$ covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$, the eigendecomposition is:

$$\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \mathbf{V} \boldsymbol{\Lambda} \mathbf{V}^{\top}$$

where $\mathbf{V} = [\mathbf{v}_1, ..., \mathbf{v}_N]$ are eigenvectors (principal components) and $\boldsymbol{\Lambda} = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, ..., \lambda_N)$ with $\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq ... \geq \lambda_N \geq 0$.

**Interpretation**:
- $\mathbf{v}_1$ (first eigenvector) is the direction of maximum variance. In equity markets, this is the "market factor" — all stocks load positively.
- $\mathbf{v}_2$ often represents a growth-vs-value or tech-vs-cyclical split.
- $\lambda_k / \sum_j \lambda_j$ is the fraction of total variance explained by PC $k$.

**Identifying hidden factor clusters**: Plot the fraction of variance explained by successive PCs. If the first 3 PCs explain 80% of FX portfolio variance, you only have 3 independent bets despite 9 pairs. The eigenvectors reveal which pairs cluster together.

**PCA risk model construction**:

$$\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \approx \sum_{k=1}^{K} \lambda_k \mathbf{v}_k \mathbf{v}_k^{\top} + \mathbf{D}$$

where we keep the top $K$ factors and $\mathbf{D}$ is a diagonal matrix of residual (idiosyncratic) variances. This gives a low-rank approximation that avoids the ill-conditioning of the full sample matrix.

```python
def pca_risk_model(returns, n_factors=3):
    """
    Construct PCA-based risk model.
    
    returns : T x N array
    n_factors : number of principal components to retain
    """
    S = np.cov(returns.T)
    eigenvalues, eigenvectors = np.linalg.eigh(S)
    
    # Sort descending
    idx = np.argsort(eigenvalues)[::-1]
    eigenvalues = eigenvalues[idx]
    eigenvectors = eigenvectors[:, idx]
    
    # Factor covariance: top K factors
    Lambda_K = np.diag(eigenvalues[:n_factors])
    V_K = eigenvectors[:, :n_factors]
    
    # Factor component
    Sigma_factor = V_K @ Lambda_K @ V_K.T
    
    # Residual (diagonal)
    D = np.diag(np.diag(S - Sigma_factor))
    D = np.maximum(D, 1e-8 * np.eye(len(S)))  # ensure positive
    
    Sigma_pca = Sigma_factor + D
    
    pct_explained = eigenvalues[:n_factors].sum() / eigenvalues.sum()
    return Sigma_pca, V_K, eigenvalues, pct_explained
```

### 4.4 Drawdown Control: Mechanics and Frameworks

**Maximum drawdown (MDD)** is the largest peak-to-trough decline in portfolio value over the full history:

$$MDD = \max_{0 \leq t \leq T} \left(\max_{0 \leq s \leq t} V_s - V_t\right) / \max_{0 \leq s \leq t} V_s$$

MDD is a backwards-looking diagnostic. For prospective risk management, we use **rolling drawdown**: the current drawdown from the most recent peak.

**Proportional scaling on drawdown**: A common approach scales exposure as drawdown increases:

$$\text{Scale}_{DD} = \max\left(0, 1 - \frac{DD_t}{DD_{max}}\right)$$

If the strategy draws down 15% and the limit is 30%, scale to 50% of normal size. At the limit, scale to 0% (risk-off).

**Hard thresholds**: Simply halt trading if rolling drawdown exceeds a limit (e.g., 20%). Restart with a defined cool-down period and recovery criteria. This is crude but prevents catastrophic loss of capital.

**The Calmar Ratio**: $\text{Calmar} = \text{Annual Return} / \text{MDD}$. A Calmar > 1.0 is good; > 2.0 is excellent for systematic strategies.

### 4.5 Stress Testing

Three categories of stress test:

1. **Historical scenarios**: Replay specific market episodes (2008 GFC, 2020 COVID crash, 1998 LTCM, 2022 rate shock). Requires that positions are fully mapped to instruments and that historical P&L can be reconstructed. The key value is identifying correlations that spike in crisis but appear benign in normal periods.

2. **Monte Carlo simulation**: Generate thousands of return scenarios by sampling from a fitted distribution (e.g., multivariate normal with GARCH volatility, or by block-bootstrapping historical returns to preserve autocorrelation and fat tails). Compute the portfolio return distribution and read off tail statistics.

3. **Adversarial / factor shock scenarios**: Manually shock individual factors: "what if carry unwinds 3σ simultaneously?" or "what if USD rallies 5% in a single day?" This requires a factor model of the portfolio. Adversarial scenarios are chosen based on the portfolio's known vulnerabilities (identified by factor decomposition).

## Section 5 — Machine Learning for Systematic Investing

### 5.1 Why Finance Is Different: The ML Challenge

Finance presents ML practitioners with challenges not found in computer vision or NLP:

**Small signal-to-noise ratio**: The Sharpe ratio of most true return predictors is in the range 0.3–0.8. The signal embedded in data is a tiny fraction of the noise. Monthly equity returns have vol ≈ 20% annualised but expected premia are 3–8% — signal is 15–40% of noise. Compare to image classification where signal/noise is effectively infinite.

**Non-stationarity**: Financial data is decidedly not stationary. The distribution of returns, correlations, and factor loadings changes over time due to structural economic change, regulatory shifts, market microstructure evolution, and changing investor composition. A model trained on 2000–2010 data faces a fundamentally different market regime in 2020–2025 (ZIRP → hiking cycle, passive investing dominance, algorithmic market-making).

**Adversarial dynamics**: When a trading signal is discovered and deployed, it changes market prices in ways that reduce the alpha. Unlike Google Images, the "test set" actively adapts to the model. Strategies degrade as they become crowded — momentum crashes (Barroso-Santa-Clara 2015), value compression (2017–2020), and carry drawdowns in FX all reflect this.

**Overfitting risk (the multiple testing problem)**: With enough trials, any pattern can be found in any dataset. The number of possible financial signals is effectively unlimited (technical indicators, macro variables, alternative data, all their combinations). This requires statistical discipline far beyond what is typical in ML.

### 5.2 Feature Engineering for Financial ML

**Price-based features**:
- Momentum: $r_{t-h:t} = (P_t - P_{t-h})/P_{t-h}$ for $h \in \{1, 3, 6, 12\}$ months
- Volatility: $\sigma_t = \text{std}(r_{t-20:t}) \times \sqrt{252}$
- Momentum/vol ratio: $r_{t-12:t-1} / \sigma_t$ (risk-adjusted momentum)
- RSI: $100 - 100/(1 + RS)$ where $RS$ = avg gains / avg losses over N periods
- Distance from moving average: $(P_t - MA_t^{200}) / \sigma_t$ (Bollinger Z-score)

**Fundamental features** (equities):
- Book-to-market, earnings yield, cash flow yield
- Return on equity, gross profitability
- Asset growth, investment rate, accruals
- All features require point-in-time construction (report date alignment)

**Macro features** (FX):
- Interest rate differentials (carry)
- Real exchange rate vs. PPP (purchasing power parity value)
- Current account balance / GDP
- CPI differential, output gap differential
- Policy rate uncertainty (implied vol from OIS curve)

**Transformations**:
- Z-score cross-sectionally (within each asset class on each date)
- Log transformation for price/ratio variables with positive support
- Lag features: add $x_{t-1}, x_{t-2}$ to capture momentum-of-momentum
- Rolling correlation as a feature: "is carry correlated with momentum now?"

### 5.3 Proper Train/Validation/Test Splits

**Why random splits are wrong**: Financial returns are autocorrelated. If you randomly assign observations to train/test, future data leaks into training (a Monday in November 2024 in train; Tuesday in test). The model learns spurious patterns from looking at adjacent time periods.

**Walk-forward validation** (expanding window):
- Train on $[0, t_1]$, validate on $[t_1, t_1+\Delta]$
- Train on $[0, t_1+\Delta]$, validate on $[t_1+\Delta, t_1+2\Delta]$
- ... repeat until the full dataset is covered
- Choose hyperparameters that optimise mean validation performance

**Purging and embargoing**: Even with time-series splits, there is leakage if the return being predicted (e.g., 21-day return ending at $t+21$) overlaps with the training period. Specifically:
- **Purge**: Remove training samples whose label overlaps with the test period.
- **Embargo**: Remove the first $k$ samples after the training cutoff (where $k$ equals the label horizon) to avoid correlation.

**Combinatorial Purged Cross-Validation (CPCV)** (López de Prado 2018): Generate all $\binom{N}{k}$ combinations of $k$ test folds from $N$ total folds, with purging and embargo applied. This gives a better estimate of the distribution of backtest paths, enabling statistical testing of whether the strategy beats a benchmark.

```python
def walk_forward_cv(X, y, n_splits=5, purge_days=21, embargo_days=5):
    """
    Walk-forward cross-validation with purging and embargo.
    
    X : feature DataFrame with DatetimeIndex
    y : target Series with DatetimeIndex
    purge_days : days to remove before test period (label overlap)
    embargo_days : days to remove after test period
    """
    n = len(X)
    fold_size = n // (n_splits + 1)
    
    splits = []
    for i in range(1, n_splits + 1):
        test_start = i * fold_size
        test_end = (i + 1) * fold_size
        
        # Purge: remove last purge_days from training
        train_end = test_start - purge_days
        train_idx = list(range(0, train_end))
        
        # Test indices
        test_end_embargoed = min(test_end + embargo_days, n)
        test_idx = list(range(test_start, test_end))
        
        splits.append((train_idx, test_idx))
    
    return splits
```

### 5.4 Regularisation: LASSO, Ridge, Elastic Net

When features $N$ is comparable to observations $T$, OLS overfits catastrophically. Regularisation constrains the model to reduce variance at the cost of bias.

**Ridge regression (L2)**:
$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}^{Ridge} = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}\|^2 + \lambda \|\boldsymbol{\beta}\|^2$$

Closed form: $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}^{Ridge} = (\mathbf{X}^{\top}\mathbf{X} + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1} \mathbf{X}^{\top} \mathbf{y}$

Ridge shrinks all coefficients toward zero but never sets them exactly to zero. It handles collinear features well (distributes weight among correlated predictors).

**LASSO regression (L1)**:
$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}^{LASSO} = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}\|^2 + \lambda \|\boldsymbol{\beta}\|_1$$

LASSO performs automatic variable selection — it sets many coefficients to exactly zero (sparse solution). Excellent for high-dimensional feature spaces where only a small number of signals are truly predictive.

**Elastic Net** combines both penalties:
$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}^{EN} = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}\|^2 + \lambda_1 \|\boldsymbol{\beta}\|_1 + \lambda_2 \|\boldsymbol{\beta}\|^2$$

Elastic net inherits LASSO's sparsity with Ridge's handling of correlated features. It is the preferred regularised regression for financial ML with moderate feature counts (20–200 features).

**Hyperparameter selection**: Use walk-forward cross-validation (not k-fold) on the validation set to choose $\lambda$ (or $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$). The optimal regularisation is often stronger than expected — in finance, underfit models outperform overfit ones OOS.

### 5.5 Gradient Boosting for Tabular Financial Data

Gradient boosting (Friedman 2001) builds an ensemble of shallow decision trees sequentially, where each tree fits the *residuals* of the ensemble so far:

$$\hat{y} = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \gamma_m h_m(\mathbf{x})$$

where each $h_m$ is a shallow tree trained to minimise the residual loss.

**Why gradient boosting outperforms neural networks for tabular financial data**:
1. **Invariant to feature scaling**: Financial features have wildly different scales; GBM handles this naturally, neural nets require careful normalisation.
2. **Handles missing data**: Directly via NaN-direction assignment in tree splits.
3. **Feature interactions**: Automatically captures interactions between features (e.g., carry is predictive only when momentum is aligned).
4. **Better with small datasets**: Financial datasets are often 1,000–50,000 rows; GBM uses resources more efficiently than deep NNs.
5. **Interpretability**: Feature importance and SHAP values readily available.

**XGBoost vs. LightGBM**: LightGBM is faster (histogram-based splitting, leaf-wise growth) and handles categorical features natively. For FX signal prediction with a small universe, both give similar results; LightGBM trains faster.

Key hyperparameters for financial applications:
- `max_depth`: 3–5 (shallow trees prevent overfitting)
- `learning_rate`: 0.01–0.05 (small with many trees)
- `n_estimators`: 200–1000 (with early stopping on validation set)
- `subsample`: 0.5–0.8 (bagging — further reduces overfitting)
- `min_child_weight`: 5–20 (minimum samples per leaf — critical for small datasets)

```python
import lightgbm as lgb
from sklearn.metrics import mean_squared_error

def train_gbm_signal(X_train, y_train, X_val, y_val):
    """Train LightGBM for return prediction with early stopping."""
    params = {
        'objective': 'regression',
        'metric': 'rmse',
        'num_leaves': 15,
        'max_depth': 4,
        'learning_rate': 0.02,
        'n_estimators': 1000,
        'subsample': 0.7,
        'colsample_bytree': 0.7,
        'min_child_samples': 10,
        'reg_alpha': 0.1,   # L1
        'reg_lambda': 1.0,  # L2
        'verbose': -1,
    }
    
    callbacks = [lgb.early_stopping(50), lgb.log_evaluation(100)]
    
    model = lgb.LGBMRegressor(**params)
    model.fit(X_train, y_train,
              eval_set=[(X_val, y_val)],
              callbacks=callbacks)
    
    return model
```

### 5.6 LSTM and Transformers for Time Series

**LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory)** architectures maintain a hidden state $h_t$ and cell state $c_t$ updated by gating mechanisms:

- **Input gate**: $i_t = \sigma(W_i [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_i)$
- **Forget gate**: $f_t = \sigma(W_f [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_f)$
- **Output gate**: $o_t = \sigma(W_o [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_o)$
- **Cell update**: $c_t = f_t \odot c_{t-1} + i_t \odot \tanh(W_c [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_c)$
- **Hidden state**: $h_t = o_t \odot \tanh(c_t)$

LSTMs can capture autocorrelation patterns in return series that a cross-sectional GBM model misses. They are most useful when the *sequence* of past values matters, not just the cross-sectional ranking.

**When LSTMs add value over GBM**: When the prediction target has strong serial patterns (e.g., volatility clustering, trending regimes), when the feature set includes time series of macro variables with complex lags, or when the sequence itself is the input (raw price path vs. pre-engineered features).

**Transformers in finance**: The attention mechanism in Transformers is powerful for NLP but underperforms LSTM on noisy financial time series in most benchmark studies. Key reasons:
1. Transformers need much more data than is available for most financial series
2. The positional encoding does not generalise well to the irregular and non-stationary nature of financial time series
3. Attention is computationally expensive relative to the information gain
4. Oxford 2025 benchmarks (Singh et al.) show LSTM with proper feature engineering matches or outperforms Transformers on FX direction prediction at 5/10-day horizons

### 5.7 The Probabilistic Sharpe Ratio and Deflated Sharpe Ratio

**Probabilistic Sharpe Ratio (PSR)** — Bailey and López de Prado (2012)

A backtest Sharpe ratio of 1.5 sounds great. But if estimated from 36 months of data, how confident are we it's truly above 0 (or above some benchmark)?

The PSR accounts for estimation error, skewness, and kurtosis in the Sharpe estimate:

$$PSR(\widehat{SR}^*) = \Phi\left(\frac{(\widehat{SR} - \widehat{SR}^*)\sqrt{T-1}}{\sqrt{1 - \hat{\gamma}_3 \widehat{SR} + \frac{\hat{\gamma}_4 - 1}{4}\widehat{SR}^2}}\right)$$

where $\Phi$ is the normal CDF, $\hat{\gamma}_3$ is sample skewness, $\hat{\gamma}_4$ is excess kurtosis, $T$ is the number of returns, and $\widehat{SR}^*$ is the benchmark (typically 0 or a hurdle).

Interpretation: PSR(0) = 97% means there is a 97% probability that the true SR > 0, accounting for non-normality in returns. A strategy with fat-tailed losses (negative skewness, positive kurtosis) has lower PSR than a strategy with the same Sharpe but more normal returns.

**Deflated Sharpe Ratio (DSR)** — Bailey and López de Prado (2014)

When a strategy is selected from $N$ tried configurations, the maximum Sharpe from random selection is:

$$E[\widehat{SR}^*_{max}] \approx (1 - \gamma) Z^{-1}\left(1 - \frac{1}{N}\right) + \gamma Z^{-1}\left(1 - \frac{1}{Ne}\right)$$

where $\gamma \approx 0.5772$ (Euler-Mascheroni constant) and $Z^{-1}$ is the inverse normal CDF.

The DSR tests whether the selected strategy's SR is significant *after* accounting for the multiple trials: $PSR(E[\widehat{SR}^*_{max}])$.

For a repository of 27 strategy variants (typical in a systematic research process), the expected maximum SR from noise alone at T=36 months is approximately 1.35. A strategy needs SR > 1.35 just to exceed what noise would produce by random selection among 27 trials.

## Section 6 — Signal Combination: Institutional Practices

### 6.1 Equal-Weighting: When It Dominates

The simplest combination: $S_{combined} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K} z_k$

Equal-weighting consistently beats IC-weighted combinations when:
1. **Small K**: With fewer than 10 signals, estimation error in IC estimates dominates — the difference between a signal with true ICIR=0.4 and 0.5 is not detectable in 3 years of data, but you will act on the noise.
2. **Short OOS history**: Less than 2 years of live IC data — prior estimate is too noisy.
3. **Unstable ICs**: If signal ICs vary dramatically regime-to-regime, average IC is a poor estimator of forward IC.
4. **Correlated signals**: If signals are highly correlated, the effective $K$ is smaller and IC-weighting matters even less.

The DeMiguel (2009) result that 1/N beats MVO in equity portfolios generalises: in signal combination, equal-weight beats IC-weighted when estimation error is high relative to true IC variation across signals.

### 6.2 IC-Weighted Combination: When It Adds Value

IC-weighting assigns weight proportional to each signal's historical ICIR:

$$w_k^{IC} = \frac{ICIR_k}{\sum_{j} ICIR_j}$$

$$S_{combined} = \sum_k w_k^{IC} z_k$$

This adds value when:
- $K \geq 10$ signals: enough signals to reliably rank them
- Long track record: ICIR estimates based on 5+ years of data
- ICIR spreads are large: some signals clearly dominate others (ICIR of 0.8 vs. 0.2)

In practice, even pure IC-weighting should be shrunk toward equal weight:

$$w_k^{shrunk} = (1-\alpha) \cdot w_k^{IC} + \alpha \cdot \frac{1}{K}$$

with $\alpha \approx 0.5$ for small K.

### 6.3 Turnover-Adjusted IR: The True Net-of-Cost Information Ratio

Trading costs reduce the realised IR of a signal. A fast-decaying signal requires high turnover, which destroys net returns. The turnover-adjusted formula:

$$IR_{adj} = \frac{IC \times \sqrt{BR}}{1 + 2 \cdot TC \cdot \lambda}$$

where $TC$ is the one-way transaction cost per trade and $\lambda$ is the signal turnover rate (fraction of portfolio rebuilt per period). Derivation: if each signal position costs $TC$ to enter and $TC$ to exit, and fraction $\lambda$ of the portfolio turns over each period, the cost drag is approximately $2 \cdot TC \cdot \lambda$ per period.

For FX with spread costs of 1–3bps and monthly rebalancing (low turnover), the cost drag is minimal. For equity momentum with daily rebalancing and 5bps costs, the drag can eliminate 30–50% of gross IR.

**The buy-hold spread** (Novy-Marx and Velikov 2016): Instead of trading to the exact signal, use asymmetric thresholds. Enter a position when signal score exceeds $z_{entry}$ and only exit when it falls below $z_{exit} < z_{entry}$. This reduces turnover substantially (30–50%) at the cost of slightly stale positions. The net-of-cost improvement is typically 10–20% in IR.

### 6.4 The AQR Signal Combination Approach

AQR (Asness, Moskowitz, Pedersen) uses a stylised but powerful approach documented in their academic work:

1. **Within-factor z-scoring**: For each signal $k$, compute cross-sectional z-scores at each date.
2. **Winsorise**: Cap at ±3σ to prevent outliers from dominating.
3. **Equal-weight across factors**: Within a broad factor category (e.g., "value"), equal-weight all value sub-signals; then equal-weight across factor categories.
4. **Risk-weight across assets**: Position size $\propto 1/\sigma_i$ where $\sigma_i$ is the asset's volatility. This implements vol normalisation within the long-short portfolio.
5. **Volatility target the portfolio**: Scale aggregate exposure to 10–15% target vol.

The power of this approach is its parsimony — very few choices to overfit — and its robustness to individual signal failures.

## Section 7 — Backtesting Standards and Pitfalls

### 7.1 The Taxonomy of Look-Ahead Bias

Look-ahead bias is the most common and consequential error in strategy research. It occurs when information unavailable at the time of the trading decision is used in the backtest.

**Type 1: Survivorship bias** — Using a current-day universe that only contains companies that survived. Eliminates all the stocks that went bankrupt, were taken private, or were delisted. Inflates backtest returns by 1–3% per year in equities. Fix: use point-in-time universe construction from CRSP or similar.

**Type 2: Point-in-time data errors** — Using the *revised* value of a data series rather than the *initially released* value. GDP data is revised 3–5 times; using the final vintage for a 2010 backtest uses data nobody had in 2010. Fix: use vintaged databases (ALFRED for macro, Compustat quarterly with proper filing dates for fundamentals).

**Type 3: Timestamp misalignment** — Signal computed at $t$ using returns that include information from $t$. Example: computing momentum as of close on day $t$ and entering the trade at open on day $t$ (the close-to-close return on day $t$ is not known at the open). Fix: always shift signals by at least one full trading day.

**Type 4: Implicit foresight in parameter calibration** — Choosing parameters (e.g., lookback window, signal weight) by optimising on the full sample and then evaluating on the same sample. Fix: walk-forward parameter estimation; or if fixed parameters are used, they must be chosen without reference to the specific test dataset.

**Type 5: Filling survivorship-biased benchmarks** — Comparing a strategy to an index that has had constituents retroactively changed. Example: testing against "S&P 500" using current 500 members rather than the index as it existed historically.

### 7.2 The Multiple Testing Problem and Minimum t-Statistics

Harvey, Liu, and Zhu (2016) surveyed 315 published factor papers through 2012. They showed that:
- The standard t > 2.0 threshold used in most papers corresponds to a 5% false discovery rate in a single test
- With 315 factors tried, the expected number of false discoveries at t > 2.0 is substantial
- The proper threshold, accounting for multiple testing, should be t > 3.0 for new factors presented in 2016, and rising over time

**Bonferroni correction**: If you run $M$ tests and want family-wise error rate < 5%:
$$t_{threshold} = z_{1 - 0.05/(2M)}$$

For M = 100 tests, the threshold is approximately t > 4.4. This is extremely conservative (assumes all tests are independent).

**Benjamini-Hochberg (FDR control)**: Less conservative; controls the **expected proportion** of false discoveries among rejections (False Discovery Rate). Sort p-values $p_{(1)} \leq p_{(2)} \leq ... \leq p_{(M)}$. Find the largest $k$ such that $p_{(k)} \leq k\alpha/M$. Reject all hypotheses up to $k$.

**Novy-Marx (2015)**: Even with honest testing, the universe of possible financial signals is so vast that a reasonably sophisticated researcher will find many "significant" signals that are pure noise. The discipline needed: (1) preregistration of the hypothesis before looking at data; (2) Deflated Sharpe Ratio evaluation; (3) economic rationale; (4) OOS validation on a held-out period.

### 7.3 Parameter Sensitivity Analysis: ±30% Perturbation

A robust strategy should work across a *neighbourhood* of parameter values, not just the exact optimum. Standard protocol:

For each key parameter $\theta$ (lookback window, rebalancing frequency, signal weight):
1. Test the strategy at $0.7\theta$, $0.85\theta$, $\theta$, $1.15\theta$, $1.3\theta$
2. Compute Sharpe ratio at each setting
3. A valid strategy should show monotone or dome-shaped Sharpe with no cliff-edges

If the strategy works at lookback=12 months but fails at 11 and 13, it is overfitted to the specific value.

```python
def parameter_sensitivity(strategy_fn, base_params, param_name, 
                           perturbations=[-0.3, -0.15, 0, 0.15, 0.30]):
    """Test strategy robustness across parameter perturbations."""
    results = {}
    base_val = base_params[param_name]
    
    for pct in perturbations:
        params = base_params.copy()
        params[param_name] = base_val * (1 + pct)
        returns = strategy_fn(**params)
        sr = returns.mean() / returns.std() * np.sqrt(252)
        results[f'{pct:+.0%}'] = sr
    
    return pd.Series(results, name=f'Sharpe vs {param_name}')
```

## Section 8 — Transaction Costs and Implementation

### 8.1 The Almgren-Chriss Market Impact Model

Almgren and Chriss (2001) derived the optimal execution trajectory that minimises the expected implementation shortfall plus a penalty for variance of execution cost.

**Market impact decomposition**:

$$\text{Total Cost} = \underbrace{\eta \cdot v^2}_{\text{temporary impact}} + \underbrace{\gamma \cdot v}_{\text{permanent impact}}$$

where $v$ is the rate of trading (shares per unit time), $\eta$ is the temporary impact coefficient (captures market maker's adverse selection cost), and $\gamma$ is the permanent impact coefficient (captures fundamental information content of the trade).

**Temporary impact** (liquidity provision): Proportional to the *rate* of trading. Moves prices against you transiently; prices recover after trading ceases. Related to the bid-ask spread and short-term elasticity.

**Permanent impact**: Proportional to *total* shares traded. Reflects the information content of the trade — if trading 1% of daily volume, the permanent price move is approximately proportional. This does not reverse.

For a trade of size $X$ to be completed in time $T$, the optimal strategy is:

$$x(t) = X \cdot \frac{\sinh(\kappa(T-t))}{\sinh(\kappa T)}$$

where $\kappa = \sqrt{\eta/\gamma}$ characterises the relative importance of temporary vs. permanent impact. In the limit $\kappa \to 0$ (no temporary impact), trade uniformly (TWAP). In the limit $\kappa \to \infty$ (no permanent impact), execute as fast as possible (limit order).

For FX spot markets: permanent impact is negligible (FX markets are highly liquid), so the relevant cost is the spread plus temporary impact during large trades. For EUR/USD (daily volume > $600bn), the spread is 0.2–0.5bps and temporary impact for sizes up to $100m is < 1bp.

### 8.2 Implementation Shortfall: Perold (1988)

Implementation shortfall (IS) measures the gap between the paper portfolio return (at decision price) and the actual portfolio return (at execution prices):

$$IS = \underbrace{(P_{exec} - P_{decision}) \times Q}_{execution\ cost} + \underbrace{(P_{decision} - P_{open}) \times Q}_{market\ impact}$$

Decomposed into:
- **Delay cost**: Cost of not acting immediately (price moves while you are deciding or waiting)
- **Market impact**: Price moves caused by your own trading
- **Spread cost**: Bid-ask spread paid
- **Opportunity cost**: Return on shares you intended to buy but did not (partial fills)

IS matters most for equity long-short strategies where positions are in illiquid stocks. For G10 FX, IS is primarily the spread cost plus delay cost (execution is near-instantaneous for spot FX in normal markets).

### 8.3 FX vs. Equity Transaction Costs

| Asset Class | Typical Round-Trip Cost | Turnover Capacity | Signal Half-Life |
|------------|------------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| EUR/USD spot | 0.4–1.0 bps | Very high | Days to weeks |
| EM FX spot | 5–30 bps | Moderate | Weeks to months |
| US large-cap equity | 5–15 bps (incl. impact) | High | Months |
| US small-cap equity | 30–100 bps | Low | Months |
| Liquid futures | 1–3 bps | Very high | Days to months |

**Key implication**: FX strategies can profitably use signals with shorter half-lives than equity strategies. A signal with a 2-week half-life and monthly gross IC of 0.03 has:
- In FX (1bp cost): Net IC = 0.03 − 0.01 ≈ 0.02 (viable)
- In small-cap equity (50bp cost): Net IC = 0.03 − 0.25 (destroyed)

This is why many quant funds run their fastest signals in FX and futures, while running slower fundamental signals in equity.

### 8.4 VWAP vs. TWAP Execution

**TWAP (Time-Weighted Average Price)**: Execute $1/N$ of the order every time unit over $N$ units.
- Simple, predictable
- Appropriate when market impact is proportional to rate (not size), and when you have no view on intraday price path
- Used when the goal is to reduce variance of execution price

**VWAP (Volume-Weighted Average Price)**: Execute in proportion to the expected intraday volume profile (typically U-shaped: high at open and close).
- Matches your participation rate to market volume
- Achieves VWAP benchmark commonly used for institutional equity orders
- More appropriate for large equity orders where spread cost and market timing matter
- For FX: VWAP is less relevant (24-hour market); instead, avoid execution at illiquid times (Asia open on thin markets)

## Section 9 — Alternative Data: A Systematic Framework

### 9.1 The Alternative Data Taxonomy

| Category | Examples | Signal Type | Typical Decay |
|---------|----------|-------------|--------------|
| Satellite imagery | Parking lot car counts, oil storage levels, crop health | Revenue/inventory nowcast | 2–8 weeks |
| NLP / Text | Earnings call tone, news sentiment, patent filings | Sentiment, management guidance | 1 day – 3 months |
| Credit/debit card data | Consumer spending by merchant, category | Revenue estimate | 2–6 weeks |
| App usage data | Daily active users, downloads | User growth proxy | 2–8 weeks |
| Web scraping | Job listings, pricing data, product reviews | Business momentum | 1–4 weeks |
| Supply chain / shipping | AIS ship tracking, container port activity | Trade volumes | 2–6 weeks |
| Geolocation / foot traffic | Store visits, office occupancy | Business health | 1–4 weeks |

The common theme: alternative data provides earlier and more frequent information about company fundamentals or macro variables than traditional disclosures (quarterly earnings, monthly macro releases).

### 9.2 Alpha Decay of Alternative Data vs. Traditional Factors

Alternative data signals decay faster than traditional fundamental factors:
- Satellite parking lot counts: IC may be 0.04 at 2 weeks, near 0 by 3 months
- Earnings sentiment from calls: useful for 1–2 months after the call
- Book-to-market (value): IC of 0.02 at 1 month, persists above 0.01 for 12–24 months

This means:
1. Alternative data signals require higher turnover and lower transaction costs to capture
2. They are most valuable in FX and futures where transaction costs are low
3. Their combination with slow fundamental signals creates a higher-breadth multi-factor model

### 9.3 Evaluating a New Alternative Data Source

Standard evaluation protocol:

**Step 1: Universe and coverage check**
What fraction of your investable universe does the data cover? Satellite data may cover only 60% of companies (those with large physical footprints). Coverage bias can make IC look artificially high.

**Step 2: IC test with proper methodology**
- Compute signal from data, align with company, match to forward return
- Use the report/publication date as the signal date (not the event date)
- Compute monthly IC over a backtest period (minimum 3 years, prefer 5+)
- Assess mean IC, std IC, ICIR, and t-statistic

**Step 3: Decay analysis**
Compute IC at 1-week, 2-week, 1-month, 3-month horizons. The decay profile determines optimal holding period.

**Step 4: Correlation with existing signals**
If the new signal has IC = 0.04 but correlates 0.8 with your existing momentum signal, incremental value is minimal. Compute the *conditional* IC: regress returns on existing signals plus new signal; is the new signal's coefficient significant?

**Step 5: Capacity and cost assessment**
Alternative data is expensive ($50k–$1m+ per year from vendors). It is only valuable if the net alpha (gross alpha minus data cost) is positive. Estimate capacity: if the signal works for the top 100 stocks by dollar volume, the maximum AUM supported depends on how much you can trade before market impact destroys the edge.

### 9.4 Free Alternative Data Sources

For research and smaller mandates:

- **NASA EarthData** (earthdata.nasa.gov): Satellite imagery, NDVI (vegetation index for agricultural signals), sea surface temperature, precipitation. Free via API. Useful for commodity production signals.
- **Marine Traffic / AIS (Automatic Identification System)**: Ship tracking data from the AIS public feed. VesselFinder and AIS Hub provide free historical data. Useful for trade flow signals (number of ships entering/leaving ports, oil tanker movements).
- **US Government Data**: Census Bureau (retail sales, construction), BLS (employment by industry), EIA (weekly oil inventory).
- **SEC EDGAR**: All company filings including 10-K, 10-Q, 8-K; full text available for NLP sentiment analysis.
- **GDELT Project** (gdeltproject.org): Global news event database, updated every 15 minutes. Free. Useful for geopolitical risk signals and event studies.
- **App store rankings**: SensorTower provides some free data; Apple/Google store rankings are public. Useful for tech company revenue nowcasting.

### 9.5 The Commoditisation Cycle

Alternative data signals follow a predictable lifecycle:

1. **Discovery** (Year 0–2): Quant team/researcher identifies the signal, data is expensive and poorly understood.
2. **Adoption** (Year 2–5): Several large quant funds buy the data; IC is still high because the market is not yet pricing the signal.
3. **Commoditisation** (Year 5–10): Data vendor markets broadly to hundreds of funds; the signal starts to be arbitraged away.
4. **Decay** (Year 10+): Signal IC has halved or more; alpha is now primarily in execution (who can act on the data fastest) rather than signal quality.

The implication: the half-life of an alternative data signal's alpha is roughly 5–7 years from discovery. By the time an academic paper is published about an alt-data signal, you are typically in the early-to-mid adoption phase.

### 9.6 NLP on Earnings Calls: FinBERT vs. Dictionaries

**Sentiment dictionaries**: Loughran-McDonald (2011) developed a finance-specific word list (positive/negative words in the financial context). Simple bag-of-words: count positive and negative words, compute net sentiment.
- Advantage: transparent, fast, long track record, easy to replicate
- Limitation: context-free (can't distinguish "not good" from "good")

**FinBERT** (Yang et al. 2019): BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) fine-tuned on financial text. Understands context and negation.
- Advantage: higher accuracy on nuanced financial language
- Limitation: computationally expensive, harder to interpret, requires fine-tuning data

Empirically, FinBERT outperforms dictionaries in IC by approximately 20–30% on earnings call text (Huang et al. 2022). The gap is largest for ambiguous statements that require context understanding. For straightforward positive/negative language, the dictionary approach is nearly as good.

## Section 10 — Performance Attribution and Strategy Evaluation

### 10.1 Fama-MacBeth Regression

Fama and MacBeth (1973) developed a two-step procedure to estimate factor risk premia in the cross-section while accounting for error-in-variables.

**Step 1: Time-series regressions (first pass)**
For each asset $i$, regress its returns on factor returns to estimate factor loadings (betas):

$$r_{it} = \alpha_i + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \hat{\beta}_{ik} f_{kt} + \epsilon_{it}$$

Run for each asset over the full time series. Collect $\hat{\beta}_{ik}$ for all $i, k$.

**Step 2: Cross-sectional regressions (second pass)**
For each time period $t$, run a cross-sectional regression of returns on estimated betas:

$$r_{it} = \lambda_{0t} + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \lambda_{kt} \hat{\beta}_{ik} + \eta_{it}$$

This gives a time series of risk premium estimates $\hat{\lambda}_{kt}$ for each factor $k$.

**Inference**: Average the $\hat{\lambda}_{kt}$ over time and t-test against zero:

$$\bar{\lambda}_k = \frac{1}{T} \sum_t \hat{\lambda}_{kt}, \quad t_k = \frac{\bar{\lambda}_k}{\sigma(\hat{\lambda}_k)/\sqrt{T}}$$

**Why Fama-MacBeth over pooled regression**: Pooled regression treats the time-series and cross-sectional dimensions symmetrically and tends to find artificially tight standard errors due to within-period error correlation. FM separates the two dimensions, and the time-series variation in $\hat{\lambda}_{kt}$ correctly accounts for the cross-period dependence in returns.

### 10.2 Factor Attribution: Decomposing Portfolio Returns

Given a factor model $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{B}\mathbf{f} + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ and portfolio weights $\mathbf{w}$:

**Portfolio factor exposures**: $\boldsymbol{\beta}_p = \mathbf{B}^{\top}\mathbf{w}$ (K-vector of portfolio loadings on each factor)

**Factor contribution to return**: $r_p^{factor} = \boldsymbol{\beta}_p^{\top}\mathbf{f}$

**Specific (idiosyncratic) return**: $r_p^{specific} = \mathbf{w}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$

**Factor contribution to risk** (Euler decomposition):
$$\sigma_p^2 = \boldsymbol{\beta}_p^{\top} \mathbf{F} \boldsymbol{\beta}_p + \mathbf{w}^{\top} \mathbf{D} \mathbf{w}$$

where $\mathbf{F}$ is the factor covariance matrix and $\mathbf{D}$ is the diagonal specific risk matrix.

Factor contribution to variance: $RC_k^{factor} = \beta_{pk} \cdot (\mathbf{F}\boldsymbol{\beta}_p)_k$, which sums to $\boldsymbol{\beta}_p^{\top}\mathbf{F}\boldsymbol{\beta}_p$.

This attribution allows monitoring: is the portfolio taking the factor risk you intended? A momentum strategy should show high loading on the MOM factor; if it shows high loading on the SMB factor, that is unintended risk.

```python
def factor_attribution(weights, factor_loadings, factor_returns, factor_cov):
    """
    Decompose portfolio return and risk into factor contributions.
    
    weights : N-vector
    factor_loadings : N x K matrix (B)
    factor_returns : T x K matrix
    factor_cov : K x K covariance matrix
    """
    # Portfolio factor exposures
    beta_p = factor_loadings.T @ weights  # K-vector
    
    # Return attribution
    factor_return_contribution = factor_returns @ beta_p  # T-vector per factor
    
    # Risk attribution
    portfolio_factor_var = beta_p @ factor_cov @ beta_p
    marginal_factor_contrib = factor_cov @ beta_p
    risk_contribution = beta_p * marginal_factor_contrib  # K-vector
    risk_pct = risk_contribution / portfolio_factor_var
    
    return {
        'beta_p': beta_p,
        'factor_risk_pct': risk_pct,
        'total_factor_var': portfolio_factor_var,
    }
```

### 10.3 IC/ICIR Monitoring: Signal Health in Real-Time

Once a strategy is live, monitor signal health monthly:

**Rolling IC**: Compute IC over a trailing 12-month window, updating monthly. Alert if rolling IC falls below half the backtest IC.

**Exponential decay fitting on live data**: As live IC data accumulates, refit the decay model:
$$IC_{live}(t) = IC_0 e^{-\lambda t}$$

If the fitted half-life shrinks significantly from the backtest half-life, the signal is decaying faster than expected — potentially due to crowding or regime change.

**IC t-statistic**: Is the live IC significantly different from zero? $t_{IC} = IC_{mean}/(\sigma_{IC}/\sqrt{T})$. If $t_{IC}$ falls below 1.5 on live data, the signal is not generating statistically meaningful predictions.

**Regime stability test**: Split live period into sub-periods by macro regime. Compare IC in each regime. If IC is strong in risk-on but near zero in risk-off, the signal has hidden regime dependence not captured in the backtest.

### 10.4 The Appraisal Ratio

The **Information Ratio** of a long-short strategy (or the active return over a benchmark) is:

$$IR = \frac{r_p - r_b}{\sigma(r_p - r_b)} = \frac{\alpha}{\sigma_\epsilon}$$

where $\alpha$ is the annualised active return (Jensen's alpha) and $\sigma_\epsilon$ is the tracking error (standard deviation of active returns).

The **Appraisal Ratio** (Treynor-Black 1973) measures alpha per unit of *specific* (idiosyncratic) risk:

$$AR = \frac{\alpha}{\sigma_\epsilon}$$

When evaluating a signal's contribution to a combined portfolio, use the appraisal ratio: a signal with high alpha but also high specific risk contributes less than a signal with the same alpha and lower specific risk.

**The optimal weight for a signal** in a combined portfolio is proportional to its appraisal ratio squared:

$$w^* \propto AR^2 / (1 + AR^2)$$

This is the Treynor-Black optimal weight — allocate more to high-appraisal-ratio signals, with diminishing returns as AR becomes very large.

### 10.5 Regime Analysis for Performance Attribution

For a FX systematic strategy, the key regime dimensions are:

**1. Rate environment**: ZIRP (near-zero rates), hiking cycle, cutting cycle. Carry strategies perform best in stable low-vol rate environments and worst during rapid hiking (as forward markets price in rate expectations, the carry signal becomes crowded and mean-reverting).

**2. Risk sentiment**: Risk-on (EM currencies and high-yielders outperform) vs. risk-off (USD, JPY, CHF outperform). Carry tends to be risk-on beta. If your strategy has unintended risk-sentiment exposure, it will underperform when risk-off hits.

**3. Dollar regime**: Dollar trending (2014–2015, 2022) vs. range-bound (2016–2019, 2023–2025). Trend/momentum signals perform better in trending regimes.

**4. Volatility regime**: Low-vol (VIX < 15) vs. high-vol (VIX > 25). Most FX signals generate better IC in low-vol regimes; high-vol periods see correlations spike and signals become less reliable.

**Implementation**: Fit a 2-state Hidden Markov Model on realised FX volatility and VIX. Compute IC conditional on each state. Alternatively, use simple quintile sorts on lagged VIX level and compute IC conditional on each quintile.

```python
from hmmlearn import hmm
import numpy as np

def fit_vol_regimes(vol_series, n_states=2):
    """
    Fit Hidden Markov Model to identify volatility regimes.
    
    vol_series : array of realised volatility observations
    Returns: state sequence and model parameters
    """
    model = hmm.GaussianHMM(n_components=n_states, covariance_type="full",
                             n_iter=100, random_state=42)
    obs = vol_series.values.reshape(-1, 1)
    model.fit(obs)
    states = model.predict(obs)
    
    # Identify which state is "low vol" and which is "high vol"
    state_means = model.means_.flatten()
    low_vol_state = np.argmin(state_means)
    high_vol_state = np.argmax(state_means)
    
    return states, model, low_vol_state, high_vol_state

def compute_conditional_ic(signal, returns, regimes, n_regimes=2):
    """Compute IC conditional on each regime."""
    results = {}
    for regime in range(n_regimes):
        mask = regimes == regime
        if mask.sum() < 10:
            continue
        regime_ic = signal[mask].corrwith(returns[mask], method='spearman')
        results[f'regime_{regime}'] = regime_ic.mean()
    return results
```

### 10.6 The Fundamental Law Applied to Signal Retirement

The Fundamental Law gives us a framework for deciding when to retire a signal:

**Signal retirement criterion**: If the live ICIR falls below $ICIR_{threshold}$ (e.g., 0.20) for a sustained period (e.g., 12 consecutive months), the signal no longer contributes meaningful alpha and should be removed.

More rigorously, compute the **contribution to portfolio IR** of each signal:

$$\Delta IR_k = IR_{combined} - IR_{combined \, \setminus \, k}$$

where $IR_{combined \setminus k}$ is the portfolio IR without signal $k$. If $\Delta IR_k < 0$, the signal is hurting the portfolio due to noise and should be dropped or down-weighted.

For a small portfolio (9 FX pairs, 3–5 signals), Monte Carlo simulation of this removal test avoids overfitting to the specific OOS period: generate 1,000 synthetic return paths from the fitted return distribution, compute $\Delta IR_k$ in each path, and check the distribution.

## Glossary of Key Terms

**Alpha decay**: The reduction in a signal's predictive power at longer horizons, typically modelled as exponential decay with a half-life.

**Appraisal ratio**: Alpha divided by specific (idiosyncratic) risk; measures the quality of a signal's contribution independent of market risk.

**Breadth (BR)**: The number of independent investment decisions per year in the Fundamental Law of Active Management.

**Conditional VaR (CVaR/ES)**: Expected loss given that the loss exceeds the VaR threshold; a coherent risk measure.

**Constant correlation target**: Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage target where all off-diagonal correlations are set to the cross-sectional mean correlation.

**Deflated Sharpe Ratio**: The Probabilistic Sharpe Ratio adjusted for the maximum expected SR from multiple trials by chance.

**ERC (Equal Risk Contribution)**: Portfolio construction method where each asset contributes equally to total portfolio variance.

**Fundamental Law**: $IR = IC \times \sqrt{BR}$ — the portfolio information ratio equals signal IC times square root of breadth.

**HRP (Hierarchical Risk Parity)**: Tree-based portfolio construction using hierarchical clustering to create a quasi-diagonal covariance matrix, then recursive bisection for allocation.

**IC (Information Coefficient)**: Cross-sectional correlation between signal scores and subsequent returns; the primary measure of signal quality.

**ICIR**: Mean IC divided by standard deviation of IC over time; measures signal consistency.

**Implementation shortfall**: Total cost of implementing a paper portfolio in live markets, including spread, delay, market impact, and opportunity costs.

**Kelly criterion**: The bet size that maximises expected log wealth; equals $\mu/\sigma^2$ for a Gaussian asset.

**Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage**: Linear combination of sample covariance matrix and a structured target, with analytically optimal shrinkage intensity.

**Look-ahead bias**: Use of information unavailable at the time of the trading decision in a backtest, inflating performance.

**MVO (Mean-Variance Optimisation)**: Markowitz portfolio construction maximising expected return for a given risk level; fails OOS due to estimation error.

**Orthogonalisation**: Removing factor exposures from a signal by regressing it on factor loadings and using residuals.

**PSR (Probabilistic Sharpe Ratio)**: Probability that the true SR exceeds a benchmark, accounting for estimation error and non-normality.

**Risk parity**: Allocation methodology equalising risk contributions across assets or asset classes, typically applying leverage to lower-volatility assets.

**Shrinkage intensity**: The parameter $\alpha$ in Ledoit-Wolf controlling the blend between sample covariance and target matrix.

**Volatility targeting**: Scaling portfolio exposure inversely to estimated volatility to maintain constant ex-ante risk.

**Walk-forward validation**: Time-series cross-validation where training always precedes validation, never using future data in model selection.

**Winsorisation**: Capping extreme values in a distribution (typically at ±3σ) to reduce the influence of outliers on estimation.
